Carry On

Your next adventure awaits

Traveling can be one of the most rewarding experiences in life. However, travel can also be stressful, expensive and unpredictable. Carry On is a sister application to Alaska Airlines that offers experience and airfare bundles, making travel affordable and memorable. Carry On’s experiences offerings focus on outdoor adventure, historical executions, kid-friendly activities, locals only events, and food and cultural experiences.

Carry On partners with Alaska Air to offer discounted plane tickets if purchased with an add-on experience. Carry On supports Alaska Airlines by having users join their mileage plan program, encourages individuals to book with Alaska, rewards users for signing up with an Alaska Airlines credit card, and opens new doors of revenue and partnerships with Alaska Airlines. Carry On enhances travelers vacations and memories with unique and curated guest experiences.

The Opportunity

The primary opportunity for this project is to create a travel application that gives users discounted airfare and perks, while enhancing their vacations through unforgettable experiences. Carry On partners with Alaska Airlines to offer discounted plane tickets, when bundled with a Carry On adventure.

The secondary opportunity is to create a product from top-to-bottom. This project gives us the opportunity to conduct survey and in-person interviews, produce user experience deliverables, use those UX deliabels to build data-driven prototypes and interfaces, and finally create a tonally appropriate marketing campaign.

Project Goals

  • Collect user research using quantitative and qualitative methods
  • Synthesis user research into user experience assets
  • Build a prototype while referencing past research and user needs, motivations and pain points
  • Conduct user testing to validate prototype UI
  • Create a marketing campaign to promote application
  • Combine user research, user experience, user interface, and marketing in one comprehensive project

User Research Execution

Constructing online surveys and conducting in-person interviews gave us valuable insight into our fledgling travel app idea. One of our main goals for this project was to make as few assumptions as possible, so we could create assets, applications, and experiences that were firmly user-centric and based in research. Below you will find summaries of our quantitative and qualitative research, quotes from our participants, and our key takeaways to include in the next steps of the project.

User research main goals

Before we set out writing surveys and interviews questions, we wanted to establish our main goals for our user research. The primary goal of our user research was to discover our users needs, how an experiential travel application could benefit Alaska Airlines, and we also wanted to try our hand at synthesising data. All of our initial goals can be seen below.

User research goals | How can we serve our users and Alaska Airlines as a whole?

Quantitative survey results

After this project, I am convinced writing survey questions is an art form. Trevor and I sent out four surveys total. The first survey tested whether our experience application idea was interesting to other people. After that assumption was validated, we wanted to know the demographic makeup of our users, as well as drill down into their travel habits, travel motivations, and travel pain points.

Information from the survey was surprising and informative. Users identified that when traveling food, culture and points of interest were the highest rated activities as well as money and time being the biggest challenges associated with traveling. I was also surprised to learn that nearly half of our sample size, choose destinations by word-of-mouth. The key takeaways from our surveys identified our initial pain points and travel experiences that were rated most valuable by our users.

SCCA | In-person interviews

Now that we had our quantitative data, we wrote our first interview questions. We set up a table on the first floor of Seattle Central College, and lured students in with the promise of free cookies. We talked to over fifty students, and sought to understand their pain points while traveling, their great experiences while traveling, and how experiences at their vacation destination shaped their feelings around traveling.

From these interviews we were able to establish our five categories of experience offerings for our app: food and drink, outdoor adventure, historical excursions, locals only, and kid-friendly experiences. Affordability and connecting with friends and family was listed as the primary motivations for travel, and surprisingly, our participants generally did not use budgeting apps.

Noteworthy positive and negative experiences while traveling emerged throughout our interviews. Our participants valued comfort, safety, airline perks, and creating memories and experiences as being the most important while traveling, and transit delays, late flights, and technical mobile phone problems as being the biggest pain points.The main takeaways from our SCCA intrews can be found below.

Sea-Tac airport | In-person interviews

Our next round of interviews involved speaking directly to our target population. We made our way to the Sea-Tac light rail station to talk to travelers who had just returned to Seattle from a trip. We had a great response, with about 40 people being interviewed.

We adjusted questions from our earlier interviews, and also added new questions to our script. We wanted to know how long people planned for trips and what was it like traveling with friends and family. Our participants planned their trips at varying times, but overall it seemed to fall into four categories. Travelers planned for less than three months, three to six months, six to nine months, and more than a year. No participants interviewed planned over two years in advance.

In terms of traveling with friends and family, many of our participants reported having no conflict while traveling. However, some were more forthright and brought up scheduling conflicts, navigation struggles and general miscommunication as pain points.

Overall, many of the pain points our participants expressed were rooted in poor or miscommunication. Below is a summary of our interview findings as well as quotes from our participants.

Marketing Execution

The final step in our Carry On journey involved creating marketing collateral to inform our audience of our new application. We provided proof of concept for two social media ads, promotional cards (than can double as postcard mailers), and a promotional video featuring one of Alaska’s most popular destinations, Maui.

This step in the process felt more creative as we were able to lean into information we had gathered from our competitor analysis. From an early date, we wanted to use vibrant, aspirational, and large-scale imagery to convey adventure for Carry On’s experiences. In marketing, we focus on using destinations as the main selling point, continue the thread with Carry On colors, and use simple rectangular shapes to provide movement and contrast where necessary.

Facebook advertising

For the Facebook ad, I focused on an outdoor excursion experience, and stayed away from island and tropical imagery. I used eye-catching imagery with unique colors to catch users attention while scrolling through their feed. The ad copy for this piece directly relates to the imagery and invokes a sense of adventure. I also placed the Carry On logo in the bottom left quadrant of the ad, as the skier is looking down at that angle.

Facebook ad copy

Chase the horizon: Hit the Slopes in Anchorage, AK

With Carry On, inject some adrenaline into your next vacation by choosing from hundreds of outdoor excursions, all while earning Alaska Airlines Mileage Plan points.

Facebook Ad | Hit the slopes in Anchorage, AK

Twitter advertising

For Twitter, I wanted to highlight Carry On’s food experiences. For the location of the ad, I researched what areas in the United States were known for having a foodie scene. After settling on Hell’s Kitchen and crafting the taglines and copy, I experimented with different food images, finally landing on a delicious looking plate of sushi.

Twitter ad copy

Eat like a local: Dig into Hell’s Kitchen, NYC

With Carry On, eat your way through top-rated food tours in over 100 cities all while earning Alaska Airlines Mileage Plan points.

Twitter ad | Dig into sushi in Hell’s Kitchen, NYC

Promotional advertising cards

I created a series of promotional cards to explain how Carry On works in an easily understood way. Referencing my flow charts from the UI section, I selected what the most important parts of the app were, and I wrote copy that was easily understood. Researching where Alaska Airlines flies, and picking destinations and imagery from those locations was extremely rewarding, and I ended up featuring four destinations: Cancun, San Francisco, Napa, and Anchorage. I wanted to feature an international location to remind users that Alaska does fly to select international destinations.

Promotional video

The last piece of the marketing material was a promotional video. I knew I wanted to pick a destination that is frequently traveled by Alaska, and a destination that has universal appeal. With those parameters in mind, I settled on featuring Maui in the video. The main goal of the promotional video is to show current and potential users what types of adventures they can experience with Carry On. The video is less informative, and more inspiring, as it uses imagery and video more that interviews and text. I love working with video, and video editing programs, so this deliverable was a joy to complete.

Final thoughts

Carry On was the longest and biggest project I undertook at my time at SCCA. I am so proud that I could tackle a project from top to bottom, and produce deliverables that range from many design disciplines. I hope to create more work like this in the future. I want to work on projects that are validated by research and interviews, and not assumption. I would like to engage in competitor analysis frequently, as not to produce clones of other products and projects. Carry On allowed me the opportunity to try on many design hats, and improve my design thinking and strategy overall.
